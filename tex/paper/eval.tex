The core of
\tool{}\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ciaran-matthew-dunne/pp2lp}}
is an OCaml program of about \toolOcamlLines{} lines behind a Python command-line
driver; the rule library of \autoref{sec:encoding} is another \toolLpLines{}
lines of \lp.
%
A single module holds rule metadata: arity, argument kinds,
and an emission strategy per rule.
%
The pipeline (\autoref{fig:pipeline}) has three stages.

A lexer and a Menhir-generated parser read the \replay{} file
into a flat list of (rule, argument, goal) records.
%
This list is then transformed into a proof tree by consuming the prefix stream
and using each rule's declared arity to attach the right number of child subproofs.
%
The two irregularities noted in \autoref{sec:pp-syntax} are handled
at this stage: normalisation markers are filtered out, and the
result derivation that precedes an instance of $\rn{ALL7}/\rn{XST8}$
is reassembled into that rule's first argument.

%
Each replay becomes a \lp{} \texttt{symbol} whose statement is the goal
and whose body is a tactic script.
%
Each rule application in the \pp{} proof tree generally corresponds to an
application of the corresponding rule lemma (e.g., $\refine\;\texttt{AND2\,\_}$),
although some \pp{} rule applications require bespoke tactic applications.
%
Rules that invoke in-scope hypotheses receive their $\Prf$-argument
by hypothesis lookup at the OCaml level; rules that introduce an antecedent
or a binder push an \texttt{assume}; result derivations are emitted as
explicit $\Res$-terms and passed as an argument; and arithmetic solver
side-conditions get proof-terms generated at translation time
(\autoref{sec:enc-rules}).
%
The resulting symbol declaration is written to a \lpf{} file which
may then be type-checked via \texttt{lambdapi check}.
%
For debugging, \tool{} records a table from each \lp{} line back to the
replay step and \pp{} goal that produced it, so a failed check reports the
offending line, its replay step, and the rule lemma's signature together.

\subsection{Benchmarks}\label{sec:bench}

We exercise \tool{} on four suites (\autoref{tab:results}).
%
\texttt{og} is a fixed set of \ogReplayed{} replays that served as an
initial development corpus;
%
\texttt{prv} validates custom proof-rules from real \ab{} developments;
%
\texttt{claude} is a synthetic suite authored by an LLM agent (Claude
Opus~4.8);
%
and \texttt{apero} samples raw industrial obligations from CLEARSY's public
proof-obligation dataset~\cite{deharbe2022pog}, translated from its POG
format into \pp's rule-validation input.
%

On the rule-validation and synthetic suites \tool{} reconstructs and
type-checks every replay but \claudeFailed{}: \texttt{og} and \texttt{prv}
in full, and \claudePass{} of \texttt{claude}'s \claudeReplayed{}, the
exceptions being goals that reach shapes we do not yet support (INS
contradiction search, nonlinear arithmetic, one result form).
%
\numRulesFired{} of the \numRulesCovered{} encoded rules fire across these
replays.
%
Re-emitting and re-checking the three suites takes \benchWallAll{} of
wall-clock time on \benchMachine{} (\benchCpuAll{} of \lp{} checking).

\texttt{apero} stresses the pipeline at industrial scale: a sampled
obligation carries a median of about \aperoMedianHyps{} hypothesis
predicates, yet \pp{} discharges most of them.
%
Of the \aperoReplayed{} replays \tool{} reconstructs, \aperoPass{} type-check
within the budget and a further \aperoTimeout{} exceed it on the largest
obligations; only \aperoFailed{} are genuine reconstruction failures.
%
These are also the first machine-produced replays to exercise \pp's equality
store, in surface forms --- strict comparisons, tuple memberships, relational
inverse --- that no synthetic goal had reached.


\subsection{Conclusion and Future Work}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented \tool, which checks \ab's Predicate Prover by
reconstructing its proofs in \lp.
%
Rather than postulating each rule as an axiom, we encode every \pp{} rule as
a symbol whose body proves it sound against a small axiomatic base; the
reconstructed corpus (including \ab's own rule-validation suite, arithmetic
included) type-checks against that base, appealing to an oracle only for the
membership facts \pp{} assumes of its arithmetic and boolean terms.

\paragraph{Upstream replay losses.}
Fewer replays reach \tool{} than the goals we submit (only \prvReplayed{} of
\prvGoals{} for \texttt{prv}), and the loss is upstream: for some provable
goals the \textsc{replay} utility omits the final continuation of an \rn{ALL7}
or \rn{XST8} step, and for others it aborts mid-file.
%
Both defects are detected at generation time and the affected goals dropped
and reported.
%
They fall hardest on set-extensionality and equality-prover goals, and on the
largest \texttt{apero} obligations, where \textsc{replay} discards most of the
traces \pp{} produces, so our synthetic suites under-test exactly the equality
reasoning \pp's store performs --- a gap the surviving \texttt{apero} replays
begin to close.

\paragraph{Spec mismatches.}
Encoding every rule against the specification turns the encoding into an
executable test of that document, and it does not always pass: \rn{NRM20}
records its pinned equality at a different conjunct position than
specified, and \rn{OPR1}'s result derivation does not follow the schema
laid out for it.
%
\tool{} implements the observed behaviour, and surfaces these discrepancies
as a by-product useful to \pp's maintainers.

\paragraph{Engine partiality.}
A few replay shapes are recognised but rejected: substitution rules
pinning a binder beyond the third slot of a compound binder, and the
result forms of two arithmetic rules that no replay in our suites
exercises.
%
All of these are rejected at emission with an error; none silently degrade.

\paragraph{Use of AI.}
The \texttt{claude} suite was authored by an LLM agent that also helped
develop the tool, so the suite could drift toward what the pipeline already
handles.
%
Our main safeguard is that the real-world \texttt{og} and \texttt{prv} suites
guard every change; the synthetic suite is also goal-level only, so the agent
sets the goals but not the rules \pp{} applies to them.

\paragraph{Future work.}
The most direct future work is upstream: repairing the \textsc{replay}
utility's truncations would recover the dropped goals, in particular the
equality-prover fragment.
%
On the encoding side, the binder-regrouping rules are currently
consumed at the syntax level rather than emitted
(\autoref{sec:enc-formulas}); deriving the regrouping isomorphisms over
tuples would let \tool{} emit them like any other rule and drop its one
syntax-level normalisation.
%
Finally, this work folds \pp{} into the \dedukti{} interoperability
ecosystem: a natural next step within the ICSPA project is exporting
checked \ab{} proofs to set-theory-based assistants such as TLA$^+$'s
TLAPS, Isabelle/ZF, and Megalodon.
