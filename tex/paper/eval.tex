\section{Implementation and Evaluation}\label{sec:eval}

The core of
\tool{}\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ciaran-matthew-dunne/pp2lp}}
is an OCaml program of about \toolOcamlLines{} lines behind a Python command-line
driver; the rule library of \autoref{sec:encoding} is another \toolLpLines{}
lines of \lp.
%
The pipeline (\autoref{fig:pipeline}) has three stages.

A lexer and a Menhir-generated parser read the \replay{} file
into a flat list of (rule, argument, goal) records.
%
This list is then transformed into a proof tree by consuming the prefix stream
and using each rule's declared arity to attach the right number of child subproofs.
%
The two irregularities noted in \autoref{sec:pp-syntax} are handled
at this stage: normalisation markers are filtered out, and the
result derivation that precedes an instance of $\rn{ALL7}/\rn{XST8}$
is reassembled into that rule's first argument.

%
Each replay becomes a \lp{} \texttt{symbol} whose statement is the goal
and whose body is a tactic script (\autoref{fig:reconstruct}).
%
Each rule application generally becomes a \refine{} of the matching rule lemma
(e.g., $\refine\;\texttt{AND2\,\_}$), though some need bespoke tactics.
%
Rules that invoke in-scope hypotheses receive their $\Prf$-argument
by hypothesis lookup at the OCaml level; rules that introduce an antecedent
or a binder push an \texttt{assume}; result derivations are emitted as
explicit $\Res$-terms and passed as an argument; and arithmetic solver
side-conditions get proof-terms generated at translation time
(\autoref{sec:enc-rules}).
%
The resulting symbol declaration is written to a \lpf{} file which
may then be type-checked via \texttt{lambdapi check}.

\subsection{Benchmarks}\label{sec:bench}

We exercise \tool{} on four suites (\autoref{tab:results}).
%
\texttt{og} is \ogReplayed{} replays, our initial development corpus;
%
\texttt{prv} validates custom proof-rules from real \ab{} developments;
%
\texttt{claude} is a synthetic suite authored by an LLM agent (Claude
Opus~4.8);
%
and \texttt{apero} samples raw industrial obligations from CLEARSY's public
proof-obligation dataset~\cite{deharbe2022pog}, translated from its POG
format into \pp's rule-validation input.
%

On the rule-validation and synthetic suites \tool{} reconstructs and
type-checks every replay but \claudeFailed{}: \texttt{og} and \texttt{prv}
in full, and \claudePass{} of \texttt{claude}'s \claudeReplayed{}, the
exceptions reaching shapes we do not yet support (INS contradiction
search, nonlinear arithmetic, one result form).
%
\numRulesFired{} of the \numRulesCovered{} encoded rules fire across these
replays.
%
Re-emitting and re-checking these suites takes \benchWallAll{} on
\benchMachine{} (\benchCpuAll{} of \lp{} checking).

\texttt{apero} stresses the pipeline at industrial scale: a sampled
obligation carries a median of about \aperoMedianHyps{} hypothesis
predicates, yet \pp{} discharges most of them.
%
Of the \aperoReplayed{} replays \tool{} reconstructs, \aperoPass{} type-check
within the budget and a further \aperoTimeout{} exceed it on the largest
obligations; only \aperoFailed{} are genuine reconstruction failures.
%
They are also the first machine-produced replays to exercise \pp's equality
store in surface forms no synthetic goal reached: strict comparisons, tuple
memberships, relational inverse.


\subsection{Conclusion and Future Work}\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented \tool, which checks \ab's Predicate Prover by reconstructing
its proofs in \lp: rather than postulating each rule as an axiom, every \pp{}
rule is a symbol whose body proves it sound against a small axiomatic base, and
the reconstructed corpus (rule-validation suite and arithmetic included)
type-checks against that base, appealing to an oracle only for the $\mathsf{INT}$
and $\mathsf{BOOL}$ memberships \pp{} assumes.

\paragraph{Upstream replay losses.}
Fewer replays reach \tool{} than the goals we submit (only \prvReplayed{} of
\prvGoals{} for \texttt{prv}), and the loss is upstream: for some provable
goals the \textsc{replay} utility omits the final continuation of an \rn{ALL7}
or \rn{XST8} step, and for others it aborts mid-file.
%
Both are detected at generation time and the affected goals dropped and reported;
they fall hardest on set-extensionality, equality-prover goals, and the largest
\texttt{apero} obligations, where \textsc{replay} discards most of \pp's traces;
our synthetic suites thus under-test \pp's equality store --- a gap the surviving
\texttt{apero} replays begin to close.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Encoding every rule against the specification makes the encoding an executable
test of that document, which it does not always pass: \rn{NRM20} pins its
equality at a different conjunct position than specified, and \rn{OPR1}'s result
derivation departs from its schema; \tool{} implements the observed behaviour, a
by-product useful to \pp's maintainers.
%
A few replay shapes are recognised but rejected: substitution past a compound
binder's third slot, and the result forms of two arithmetic rules no suite
exercises --- always at emission with an error, never a silent degradation.

\paragraph{Use of AI.}
The \texttt{claude} suite was authored by an LLM agent that also helped build
the tool, so it could drift toward what the pipeline already handles.
%
Our safeguard: the real-world \texttt{og} and \texttt{prv} suites guard every
change, and the synthetic suite is goal-level only, so the agent sets the goals
but not the rules \pp{} applies to them.

\paragraph{Future work.}
The most direct future work is upstream: repairing \textsc{replay}'s
truncations would recover the dropped goals, especially the equality-prover
fragment.
%
On the encoding side, the binder-regrouping rules are consumed at the syntax
level, not emitted (\autoref{sec:enc-formulas}); deriving their tuple
isomorphisms would let \tool{} emit them like any other rule, dropping its last
syntax-level normalisation.
%
Finally, folding \pp{} into the \dedukti{} ecosystem opens a natural ICSPA next
step: exporting checked \ab{} proofs to set-theory assistants such as TLA$^+$'s
TLAPS, Isabelle/ZF, and Megalodon.
