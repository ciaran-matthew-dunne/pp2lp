\section{Encoding \pp{} in \lp}\label{sec:encoding}

\subsection{Encoding \pp's Predicates and Expressions}\label{sec:enc-formulas}

\pp's predicates become \lp{} propositions and its expressions \lp{} terms.
%
The encoding is shallow and single-sorted: every B expression inhabits one
domain, a set code $\iota$, so an expression is a term of type $\El\,\iota$
and a predicate a term of type $\Prop$, and we assume the standard library's
classical axioms (excluded middle and propositional extensionality).
%
The library's code $o : \Setu$, with $\El\,o ↪ \Prop$, lets a predicate also
stand where a `set' element is expected, which the conjunction lists below
rely on.

The file \texttt{B.lp} fixes the trusted base.
%
Pairing, membership, and B's application are uninterpreted constants,
\[
	{\mapsto} : \El\,\iota \to \El\,\iota \to \El\,\iota, \qquad
	{\epsilon} : \El\,\iota \to \El\,\iota \to \Prop, \qquad
	\mathsf{eapp} : \El\,\iota \to \El\,\iota \to \El\,\iota,
\]
and their only axioms are for injectivity and congruence of pairing
and set extensionality.
%
Every set-theoretic operator that survives \pp's pre-processing
(\autoref{sec:pp-syntax}) is likewise an uninterpreted constant.

\paragraph{Booleans and Integers.}
Neither is axiomatised from scratch; both are injected from the library types
$\mathbb{B}$ and $\mathbb{Z}$, each by a map with a left inverse:
\begin{align*}
	\mathsf{from\_bool}                              & : \mathbb{B} \to \El\,\iota, &
	\mathsf{from\_int}                               & : \mathbb{Z} \to \El\,\iota,   \\
	\mathsf{to\_bool}                                & : \El\,\iota \to \mathbb{B}, &
	\mathsf{to\_int}                                 & : \El\,\iota \to \mathbb{Z},   \\
	\mathsf{to\_bool}\,(\mathsf{from\_bool}\,\pv{b}) & ↪ \pv{b},                    &
	\mathsf{to\_int}\,(\mathsf{from\_int}\,\pv{z})   & ↪ \pv{z}.
\end{align*}
$\mathsf{BOOL}$ and $\mathsf{INT}$ name their images; $\mathsf{BTRUE}$,
$\mathsf{BFALSE}$, $\mathbf{0}$, and $\mathbf{1}$ are the injected library
values, and a numeral is an integer literal that \lp{} coerces into the
domain through $\mathsf{from\_int}$ automatically.

The arithmetic operators ($+$, unary $-$, and $\leq$) stay abstract over
$\El\,\iota$, so unification still recovers their operands, and take their
meaning from defining axioms that a conservative orientation turns
into reductions of $\mathsf{to\_int}$:
\begin{align*}
	x + y                               & = \mathsf{from\_int}(\mathsf{to\_int}\,x + \mathsf{to\_int}\,y), &
	\mathsf{to\_int}\,(\pv{x} + \pv{y}) & ↪ \mathsf{to\_int}\,\pv{x} + \mathsf{to\_int}\,\pv{y},             \\
	{-x}                                & = \mathsf{from\_int}(-\,\mathsf{to\_int}\,x),                    &
	\mathsf{to\_int}\,(-\pv{x})         & ↪ -\,\mathsf{to\_int}\,\pv{x},                                     \\
	x \leq y                            & = (\mathsf{to\_int}\,x \leq \mathsf{to\_int}\,y).
\end{align*}
Ground arithmetic therefore computes in $\mathbb{Z}$ while the operators stay
rigid in goals (the $\leq$ axiom has no companion reduction, landing in
$\Prop$ rather than a $\mathsf{to\_int}$ position); subtraction is sugar for
$x + (-y)$, and multiplication enters only as a literal coefficient.  \pp's arithmetic
rules are then proved from these axioms (through the integer lemmas of
\texttt{lemmas/Int.lp}), not postulated.

\paragraph{List-based Conjunction.}
\pp{} treats $\land$ as left-associative, so $p \land q \land r$ parses as
$(p \land q) \land r$; some rules peel this outermost conjunct, while others
reach a conjunct at an arbitrary position.  To serve both, we encode a
conjunction as a list that grows on the right, folded by one operator:
\begin{align*}
	\mathbb{L}^{*}         & : \Setu \to \TYPE,                                    &
	\bwand                 & : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,o \to \Prop,                          \\
	∎\,[a]                 & : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,a,                                  &
	\bwand(∎ ∷ \pv{p})     & ↪ \pv{p},                                               \\
	{∷}\,[a]               & : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,a \to \El\,a \to \mathbb{L}^{*}\,a, &
	\bwand(\pv{ps} ∷ \top) & ↪ \bwand\,\pv{ps}.
\end{align*}
$\mathbb{L}^{*}\,a$ is the type of lists over $\El\,a$ (empty $∎$, right
append $∷$), and the injective $\bwand$ folds a list of propositions
($a = o$) into their conjunction.  This conjunction stays abstract:
its link to binary $\land$ is not a rewrite rule but a separate
axiomatisation, used only inside the rules' soundness proofs.  The list form
surfaces directly in a rule's type: \rn{AND4} (\autoref{sec:pp-syntax}),
which extends a conjunction by one conjunct, reads
\[
	\rn{AND4}\,[ps : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,o]\,[p : \El\,o] : \Prf\,p
	\to \Prf\,(\bwand\,ps)
	\to \Prf\,(\bwand\,(ps ∷ p)).
\]

\paragraph{Compound Binders.}
\pp{} distinguishes the compound binder $\forall (x,y) \cdot P$ from the
nested $\forall x \cdot \forall y \cdot P$ and manipulates the former as a
unit, so we mirror it with a tuple type and quantifiers over it:
\begin{align*}
	\Tuple        & : ℕ \to \TYPE,                                  &
	\mathsf{unit} & : \Tuple\,0,                                      \\
	{⨾}           & : \Tuple\,n \to \El\,\iota \to \Tuple\,(n{+}1), &
	\prj\,[n]     & : ℕ \to \Tuple\,n \to \El\,\iota,                 \\
	\allI, \xstI  & : (\Tuple\,n \to \Prop) \to \Prop,              &
	\Dia, \Hrt    & ≔ \allI.
\end{align*}
$\Tuple\,n$ holds $n$ elements of $\El\,\iota$, built from $\mathsf{unit}$ by
right append $⨾$ and read back by $\prj$ ($\prj\,0$ the last element);
$\allI$ and $\xstI$ are the $n$-ary universal and existential; and $\Dia,
	\Hrt$ are aliases of $\allI$ that carry only a proof-search role
(\autoref{sec:pp-syntax}).
%
For instance, $\forall (x,y) \cdot (x \mapsto y) \in f$ becomes
$\allI\,(\lambda v : \Tuple\,2,\ (\prj\,1\,v \mapsto \prj\,0\,v)
	\mathrel{\epsilon} f)$, the bound pair recovered by projection.
%
Each quantifier's introduction and elimination
are axiomatised \lp's $\Pi$-types, e.g.\ for $\allI$
\[
	(\Pi v : \Tuple\,n,\ \Prf\,(P\,v)) \to \Prf\,(\allI v,\,P\,v)
	\qquad\text{and its converse.}
\]


\subsection{Encoding Rules}\label{sec:enc-rules}
%
Each \pp{} rule becomes a \lp{} symbol whose type states the rule and whose
body proves it sound.  Translating a rule's statement into that type is
positional: a conclusion $H \seq P$ becomes the result type $\Prf\,P$; a
premise $H, P \seq Q$ an argument $\Prf\,P \to \Prf\,Q$; and a side-condition
($P$ occurs in $H$) an argument $\Prf\,P$, the last supplied by \tool{} at
translation time (\autoref{sec:eval}).
%
Accordingly \rn{IMP4} (prove $P \Rightarrow Q$ by assuming $P$) takes its
premise as a function argument, while the discharge rule \rn{AXM1} (close
$P \Rightarrow Q$ once $\lnot P$ is known) takes its side-condition $\lnot P$
as a plain one:
\begin{align*}
	\rn{IMP4}\,[P\,Q : \Prop] & : (\Prf\,P \to \Prf\,Q) \to \Prf\,(P \Rightarrow Q), \\
	\rn{AXM1}\,[P\,Q : \Prop] & : \Prf\,(\lnot P) \to \Prf\,(P \Rightarrow Q).
\end{align*}


\paragraph{Result Derivations.}
A result derivation (\autoref{sec:pp-syntax}) normalises a formula to a list
of predicates whose conjunction is its normal form.  We write $\mathsf{conj}$
for the fold $\bwand$ of \autoref{sec:enc-formulas} on a non-empty list, and
$\top$ on the empty one (a discharged result).  The derivation is then a type
$\Res\,t$ pairing the list with a proof that $t$ equals $\mathsf{conj}\,ts$:
\begin{align*}
	\Res                                              & : \El\,o \to \TYPE,                                 &   & \\
	\mathsf{mk\_res}\,[t]\,(ts : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,o)   & : \Prf\,(t = \mathsf{conj}\,ts) \to \Res\,t,        &   & \\
	\mathsf{res\_ts}\,[t]\,(r : \Res\,t)              & : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,o,                                &
	\mathsf{res\_ts}\,(\mathsf{mk\_res}\,\pv{ts}\,\_) & ↪ \pv{ts},                                                \\
	\mathsf{res\_eq}\,[t]\,(r : \Res\,t)              & : \Prf\,(t = \mathsf{conj}\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,r)), &
	\mathsf{res\_eq}\,(\mathsf{mk\_res}\,\_\,\pv{h})  & ↪ \pv{h}.
\end{align*}
\noindent A result-form rule (suffixed \texttt{\_1} in replays) is a
\lp{} function over $\Res$ values.  \rn{AND4\_1} realises the combination
schema of \autoref{sec:pp-syntax}, concatenating its two children's lists;
\rn{ALL7}, one of the two rules that consume a result, takes a per-instance
derivation $\rho$ and continues under the conjunction of its result,
$\restm\,r ≔ \mathsf{conj}\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,r)$:
\[
	\begin{aligned}
		\rn{AND4\_1}\,[ps : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,o]\,[p : \El\,o]
		 & : \Res\,p \to \Res\,(\bwand\,ps) \to \Res\,(\bwand\,(ps ∷ p))                                                         \\
		 & ≔ \lambda\,r_1\,r_2,\ \mathsf{mk\_res}\,(\mathsf{concat}\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,r_1)\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,r_2))\,(\ldots)
	\end{aligned}
\]
\[
	\begin{aligned}
		 & \rn{ALL7}\,[n]\,[P : \Tuple\,n \to \Prop]\,[Q : \Prop]\ (\rho : \Pi\,v : \Tuple\,n,\ \Res\,(P\,v)) : {} \\
		 & \quad \Prf\,((\Dia v,\ \restm\,(\rho\,v)) \Rightarrow Q)
		\to \Prf\,((\allI v,\ P\,v) \Rightarrow Q)
	\end{aligned}
\]
\noindent For example, consider the result derivation below, for the body
$P_1 \land P_2 \land P_3$ with each conjunct kept at a \rn{STOP\_1} leaf:
\begin{center}\footnotesize
	\ebproofset{separation=1em}%
	\begin{prooftree}
		\infer0[\rn{STOP\_1}]{(\seq P_3) \yields [P_3]}
		\infer0[\rn{STOP\_1}]{(\seq P_2) \yields [P_2]}
		\infer0[\rn{STOP\_1}]{(\seq P_1) \yields [P_1]}
		\infer2[\rn{AND4\_1}]{(\seq P_1 \land P_2) \yields [P_2, P_1]}
		\infer2[\rn{AND4\_1}]{(\seq P_1 \land P_2 \land P_3) \yields [P_3, P_2, P_1]}
		\hypo{}
		\ellipsis{}{\seq (\Dia x \cdot P_3 \land P_2 \land P_1) \Rightarrow G}
		\infer2[\rn{ALL7}]{\seq (\forall x \cdot P_1 \land P_2 \land P_3) \Rightarrow G}
	\end{prooftree}
\end{center}
The example shows why results are lists, not conjunctions: the two nested
\rn{AND4\_1} steps concatenate their children's lists into the single flat
list $[P_3, P_2, P_1]$, which \rn{ALL7} conjoins.  Combining eagerly with
$\land$ would instead mirror the proof's nesting as $P_3 \land (P_2 \land
	P_1)$; the list defers and flattens the conjunction.
%
Reconstructed, the \rn{ALL7} step becomes the proof term
	{\small
		\[
			\rn{ALL7}\ (\lambda v,\ \rn{AND4\_1}\ \rn{STOP\_1}\ (\rn{AND4\_1}\ \rn{STOP\_1}\ \rn{STOP\_1}))\ \ldots
		\]}%
of type $\Prf\,((\allI v,\ P_1 \land P_2 \land P_3) \Rightarrow G)$, whose
continuation antecedent $\restm\,(\rho\,v)$ reduces to the conjunction of the
flat result list ($\rho$ the derivation above, $v$ elided):
\begin{align*}
	\restm\,(\rho\,v)
	 & ≡ \mathsf{conj}\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,(\rho\,v))
	\;≡\; \mathsf{conj}\,(\mathsf{concat}\,(∎ ∷ P_3)\,(∎ ∷ P_2 ∷ P_1))              \\
	 & ≡ \mathsf{conj}\,(∎ ∷ P_3 ∷ P_2 ∷ P_1) \;≡\; \bwand\,(∎ ∷ P_3 ∷ P_2 ∷ P_1) .
\end{align*}


\paragraph{Arithmetic Side-conditions.}
A few rules carry an arithmetic side-condition that \pp's own solver discharges,
leaving no subproof in the replay: \pp's contradiction terminal \rn{ARITH}
closes a goal once its accumulated $\leq$-hypotheses are jointly contradictory,
and \rn{AR4} needs
$E + F > \mathbf{0}$ (for a hypothesis $F \leq \mathbf{0}$) to conclude
$E \leq \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow R$.
%
In the encoding each side-condition is an explicit $\Prf$-argument that \tool{}
must rebuild.
%
For \rn{ARITH}, a Fourier--Motzkin elimination over the in-scope
$\leq$-hypotheses produces a \emph{Farkas certificate}~\cite{schrijver1986} ---
nonnegative
multipliers under which the scaled hypotheses sum to a contradiction such as
$\mathbf{1} \leq \mathbf{0}$ --- assembled in \lp{} from the order lemmas of
\autoref{sec:enc-formulas}.
%
For \rn{AR4}, and for the linear $\mathbb{Z}$-equalities that assembly leaves
behind (a reassociated sum, a rescaled coefficient), we use \emph{proof by
	reflection}: each side is reified to a normal form and the two compared, a
reflexive procedure we take from Coltellacci and Merz~\cite{coltellacci2025lia}.

Concretely, from $h_1 : \Prf\,(x \leq \mathbf{0})$ and
$h_2 : \Prf\,(\mathbf{1} - (x + x) \leq \mathbf{0})$, the multipliers $1$ and
$2$ add the bound of $h_2$ to twice that of $h_1$, giving
$(\mathbf{1} - (x + x)) + x + x \leq \mathbf{0}$; the generated equality
$e : \Prf\,(\mathbf{1} = (\mathbf{1} - (x + x)) + x + x)$ rewrites this to the
false $\mathbf{1} \leq \mathbf{0}$:
\[
	\mathsf{one\_not\_leq\_zero}\,\bigl(\mathsf{leq\_subst\_l}\ e\
	(\mathsf{add\_leq\_zero}\ \_\ \_\ (\mathsf{add\_leq\_zero}\ \_\ \_\ h_2\ h_1)\ h_1)\bigr)
	: \Prf\,\bot .
\]
Here $e$ is itself closed by reflection, so \pp's sum reorderings need no
bespoke permutation lemmas.


\paragraph{Soundness of the rules.}
Every rule symbol carries a checked body, not a postulate: the inference
rules are \lpmod{opaque} proofs and their result forms transparent
definitions over $\Res$, and either way \lp{} accepts the symbol only once
that body type-checks against the axiomatic base.  The proofs follow the
structure of the encoding: propositional and quantifier rules discharge to
the classical axioms and the quantifier bridges, conjunction rules to the
$\bwand$ eliminators, equality rules to congruence and substitution, and
arithmetic rules to the integer laws of \autoref{sec:enc-formulas} (and,
through them, \lp's $\mathbb{Z}$).  What remains trusted is therefore exactly
\texttt{B.lp}'s primitives: the pairing and extensionality axioms, the
boolean and integer injections with their defining axioms, the quantifier
bridges, the $\bwand$/$\Res$ interface, and a typing oracle supplying the
$\mathsf{INT}$ and $\mathsf{BOOL}$ memberships \pp{} assumes of its arithmetic
and boolean terms --- facts about variables that \pp{} types from context,
which the single-sorted encoding does not record.
