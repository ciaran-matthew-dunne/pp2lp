\section{Encoding \pp{} in \lp}\label{sec:encoding}

\subsection{Encoding Predicates and Expressions}\label{sec:enc-formulas}

We build on \lp's standard library~\cite{Blanqui2021}: object-level types are
encoded in a universe $\Setu : \TYPE$ with $\El : \Setu \to \TYPE$ mapping each
`set code' to its type of inhabitants, and $\Prop : \TYPE$ with
$\Prf : \Prop \to \TYPE$ mapping each proposition to its type of proofs.

The encoding is shallow and single-sorted: each predicate of \pp{} is a term of
type $\Prop$ and each expression is a term of type $\El\,\iota$.
%
The standard library provides symbols and axioms for the propositional
connectives and equality.
%
We also take classical axioms for excluded middle and
propositional extensionality.
%
The file \texttt{B.lp} adds constant symbols and rewrite rules for set theory,
e.g., set membership, pairing, and function application.

\paragraph{Booleans and Integers.}
The \lp{} standard library supplies a type of booleans $\mathbb{B}$,
and a type of integers $\mathbb{Z}$.
%
We embed both in the domain by the following mappings and rewrite rules:
\begin{align*}
	\mathsf{from\_bool}                              & : \mathbb{B} \to \El\,\iota &
	\mathsf{from\_int}                               & : \mathbb{Z} \to \El\,\iota   \\
	\mathsf{to\_bool}                                & : \El\,\iota \to \mathbb{B} &
	\mathsf{to\_int}                                 & : \El\,\iota \to \mathbb{Z}   \\
	\mathsf{to\_bool}\,(\mathsf{from\_bool}\,\pv{b}) & ↪ \pv{b}                    &
	\mathsf{to\_int}\,(\mathsf{from\_int}\,\pv{z})   & ↪ \pv{z}.
\end{align*}
% $\mathsf{BOOL}$ and $\mathsf{INT}$ name their images; $\mathsf{BTRUE}$,
% $\mathsf{BFALSE}$, $\mathbf{0}$, and $\mathbf{1}$ are the injected library
% values, and a numeral is an integer literal that \lp{} coerces into the
% domain through $\mathsf{from\_int}$ automatically.
%
The arithmetic operators of PP are defined by the following
equalities and rewrite rules:
%
\begin{align*}
	\mathsf{plus}\,x\,y                               & = \mathsf{from\_int}(\mathsf{to\_int}\,x + \mathsf{to\_int}\,y) &
	\mathsf{to\_int}\,(\mathsf{plus}\,\pv{x}\,\pv{y}) & ↪ \mathsf{to\_int}\,\pv{x} + \mathsf{to\_int}\,\pv{y}             \\
	\mathsf{neg}\,{x}                                 & = \mathsf{from\_int}(-\,\mathsf{to\_int}\,x)                    &
	\mathsf{to\_int}\,(\mathsf{neg}\,\pv{x})          & ↪ -\,\mathsf{to\_int}\,\pv{x}                                     \\
	\mathsf{leq}\,x\,y                                & = (\mathsf{to\_int}\,x \leq \mathsf{to\_int}\,y)                  \\
	\mathsf{sub}\,x\,y                                & = \mathsf{from\_int}(\mathsf{to\_int}\,x - \mathsf{to\_int}\,y) &
	\mathsf{to\_int}\,(\mathsf{sub}\,\pv{x}\,\pv{y})  & ↪ \mathsf{to\_int}\,\pv{x} - \mathsf{to\_int}\,\pv{y}
\end{align*}
%
The equalities are propositional so the operators do not reduce within goals,
but the rewrite rules allow computation in $ℤ$ (e.g.,
$\mathsf{to\_int}\,
	(\mathsf{plus}\,(\mathsf{from\_int}\,2)\,(\mathsf{from\_int}\,2))$
reduces to $4$).
% Subtraction is sugar for $\mathsf{plus}\,x\,(\mathsf{neg},y)$.



% Ground arithmetic therefore computes in $\mathbb{Z}$ while the operators stay
% rigid in goals ($\leq$ alone has no companion reduction, landing in $\Prop$);
%  and multiplication enters only as a literal
% coefficient.  \pp's arithmetic rules are then proved from these axioms (through
% the integer lemmas of \texttt{lemmas/Int.lp}), not postulated.

\paragraph{List-based Conjunction.}
\pp{} treats $\land$ as left-associative, so $p \land q \land r$ parses as
$(p \land q) \land r$; some rules peel this outermost conjunct, while others
reach a conjunct at an arbitrary position.
%
To serve both, we encode conjunction as a unary constructor $\bwand$
that takes a list of propositions:
\begin{align*}
	\mathbb{L}^{*}         & : \TYPE,                                      &
	\bwand                 & : \mathbb{L}^{*} \to \Prop                      \\
	∎                      & : \mathbb{L}^{*}                              &
	\bwand(∎ ∷ \pv{p})     & ↪ \pv{p}                                        \\
	{∷}                    & : \mathbb{L}^{*} \to \Prop \to \mathbb{L}^{*} &
	\bwand(\pv{ps} ∷ \top) & ↪ \bwand\,\pv{ps}
\end{align*}
%
Notably conjunctions remain abstract:
the link to binary $\land$ is not a rewrite rule but a separate
axiomatisation, used only inside the rules' soundness proofs.
%
As an example, the type of the \rn{AND4} rule from the previous section is:
\[
	\rn{AND4}\,[ps : \mathbb{L}^{*}]\,[p : \Prop] : \Prf\,p
	\to \Prf\,(\bwand\,ps)
	\to \Prf\,(\bwand\,(ps ∷ p))
\]

\paragraph{Compound Binders.}
%
The quantifier rules of \pp{} distinguish $\forall (x,y) \cdot P$ from
$\forall x \cdot \forall y \cdot P$.
%
We make use of \lp{}'s dependent types and implicit
arguments to encode tuples and quantifiers,
where $ℕ$ is the type of the natural numbers from the standard library:
%
\begin{align*}
	\Tuple        & : ℕ \to \TYPE                                   &
	\mathsf{unit} & : \Tuple\,0,                                      \\
	{⨾}\,[n : ℕ]  & : \Tuple\,n \to \El\,\iota \to \Tuple\,(n{+}1), &
	\prj\,[n : ℕ] & : ℕ \to \Tuple\,n \to \El\,\iota,                 \\
	\allI, \xstI  & : (\Tuple\,n \to \Prop) \to \Prop,              &
	\Dia, \Hrt    & ≔ \allI.
\end{align*}
%
Each term of type $\Tuple\,n$ is built from $\mathsf{unit}$ by
right append $⨾$, and $\prj$ is the projection operator.
%
The symbols $\allI$ and $\xstI$ are $n$-ary universal and existential;
and $\Dia, \Hrt$ are aliases of $\allI$.
%
For example, $\forall (x,y) \cdot (x \mapsto y) \in f$ becomes
$\allI\,(\lambda v : \Tuple\,2,\ (\prj\,0\,v \mapsto \prj\,1\,v)
	\mathrel{\epsilon} f)$.
%
Similarly to conjunction, each quantifier's introduction and elimination
rules are axioms rather than rewrite rules, e.g.:
\[
	\allI\mathsf{\_intro} : (\Pi v : \Tuple\,n,\ \Prf\,(P\,v)) \to \Prf\,(\allI v,\,P\,v)
\]

%
Each \pp{} rule becomes a \lp{} symbol whose type states the rule and whose
body is a proof script demonstrating its soundness.
%
The encodings and their soundness proofs are grouped by rule family in the
\tool{} source tree.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/ciaran-matthew-dunne/pp2lp/tree/main/lp/rules}}
%
Translating a rule's statement into that type is mostly straightforward:
%
a sequent $H \seq P$ becomes $\Prf\,P$.
%
The hypothesis list $H$ is identified with the ambient \lp{}
context, so a side-condition like ``$P$ occurs in $H$''
corresponds to an argument $\Prf\,P$.
%
For example, the \rn{AXM1} rule and its \lp{} type are as follows:
% TODO. add inference rule for AXM1 with side condition.
\begin{align*}
	\rn{AXM1}\,[P\,Q : \Prop] & : \Prf\,(\lnot P) \to \Prf\,(P \Rightarrow Q)
\end{align*}


\paragraph{Result Derivations.}
As discussed in \autoref{sec:prelim}, a result derivation gives a normal
form for a predicate as a conjunction of a list of predicates.
%
The type constructor $\Res : \Prop → \TYPE$ maps a proposition $p$ to
the type of its result derivations, where $\mathsf{mk\_res}$
pairs a list of propositions $ps$ with a proof that $p$ equals $\bwand\,{ps}$:
%
\begin{align*}
	\mathsf{mk\_res}\,[p]\,(ps : \mathbb{L}^{*})      & : \Prf\,(p = \bwand\,{ps}) \to \Res\,p,      &   & \\
	\mathsf{res\_ts}\,[p]\,(r : \Res\,ps)              & : \mathbb{L}^{*},                            &
	\mathsf{res\_ts}\,(\mathsf{mk\_res}\,\pv{ps}\,\_) & ↪ \pv{ps},                                         \\
	\mathsf{res\_eq}\,[p]\,(r : \Res\,ps)              & : \Prf\,(p = \bwand\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,r)), &
	\mathsf{res\_eq}\,(\mathsf{mk\_res}\,\_\,\pv{h})  & ↪ \pv{h}.
\end{align*}
\noindent Result-form rules (i.e., those suffixed with \texttt{\_1} in replays)
are thus \lp{} functions over $\Res$ values.
%
For example, \rn{AND4\_1} realises the combination schema of
\autoref{sec:prelim}, concatenating its two children's lists:
% \rn{ALL7}, a result-consuming rule, takes a per-instance
% derivation $\rho$ and continues under its conjunction
% $\restm\,r ≔ \mathsf{conj}\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,r)$:
\[
	\begin{aligned}
		\rn{AND4\_1}\,[ps : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,o]\,[p : \El\,o]
		 & : \Res\,p \to \Res\,(\bwand\,ps) \to \Res\,(\bwand\,(ps ∷ p))                                                         \\
		 & ≔ \lambda\,r_1\,r_2,\ \mathsf{mk\_res}\,(\mathsf{concat}\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,r_1)\,(\mathsf{res\_ts}\,r_2))\,(\ldots)
	\end{aligned}
\]
% \[
% 	\begin{aligned}
% 		 & \rn{ALL7}\,[n]\,[P : \Tuple\,n \to \Prop]\,[Q : \Prop]\ (\rho : \Pi\,v : \Tuple\,n,\ \Res\,(P\,v)) : {} \\
% 		 & \quad \Prf\,((\Dia v,\ \restm\,(\rho\,v)) \Rightarrow Q)
% 		\to \Prf\,((\allI v,\ P\,v) \Rightarrow Q)
% 	\end{aligned}
% \]
% \noindent Because results are lists, the two nested \rn{AND4\_1} steps for a
% body $P_1 \land P_2 \land P_3$ flatten into the single list $[P_3, P_2, P_1]$
% that \rn{ALL7} conjoins, where eager $\land$ would instead nest them as

\paragraph{Arithmetic Side-conditions.}
Some rules carry arithmetic side-conditions that \pp's own solver discharges
with no subproof in the replay.
%
For example, \rn{AR2} needs $n > m$ to conclude $n ≤ m ⇒ R$,
where $n$ and $m$ are numerals.
%
Fortunately, our encoding can defer to $>$ on $ℤ$ which reduces on integer literals.
%
More tricky examples are the \rn{ARITH} rule which closes a goal once
its accumulated $\leq$-hypotheses are contradictory
(e.g., $x ≤ 0$ and $1-(x+x) ≤ 0$),
%
and \rn{AR4} which needs $E + F > 0$
where $E$ and $F$ are symbolic arithmetic expressions.
%

In the encoding each side-condition is an explicit $\Prf$-argument
that our tool must rebuild.
%
For \rn{ARITH}, we use Fourier--Motzkin elimination to reduce the in-scope
$\leq$-hypotheses to a proof term that demonstrates a concrete contradiction
$1 ≤ 0$.
%
For \rn{AR4} and others, we build on the \emph{proof by reflection} technique
given by Coltellacci and Merz~\cite{coltellacci2025lia} to reduce symbolic
arithmetic expressions to a normal form.
% ^^ TODO. how does this solve the E+F>0 side condition mentioned above?

\paragraph{Proofs.}
%
Each replay becomes a \lp{} \lpkw{symbol} whose statement is the goal
and whose body is a tactic script.
%
An example is shown in \autoref{fig:reconstruct}.
%
Each rule application generally becomes a \lpkw{refine} of the matching rule lemma
(e.g., $\kw{refine}\;\texttt{AND2\,\_}$), though some need bespoke tactics.
%
Rules that invoke in-scope hypotheses receive their $\Prf$-argument
by hypothesis lookup; rules that introduce an antecedent
or a binder push an \lpkw{assume}; result derivations are emitted as
$\Res$-terms and passed as an argument; and arithmetic solver
side-conditions get proof-terms generated at translation time.
%
The resulting symbol declaration is written to a \lpf{} file which
may then be typechecked via \texttt{lambdapi check}.
