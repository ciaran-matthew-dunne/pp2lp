\section{Encoding PP's rules in LambdaPi}\label{sec:encoding}

\subsection{The trusted base}
In \lp, propositions are terms of a type \(\Prop\), and \(\Prf\) maps a
proposition to the type of its proofs (\(\Prf : \Prop \to \mathsf{TYPE}\)).
\bmethod{} expressions are terms of \(\El\,\iota\), where \(\iota\) is the
universal domain of \bbook{} set theory. A single module, \texttt{B.lp},
fixes this kernel: it reuses the logical connectives from \lp's standard
library and adds the \bbook{} primitives our proofs need---set membership
\(\,\epsilon\,\), the maplet \(\,\mapsto\,\), equality, the Boolean
elements, and just enough of a discretely ordered abelian group on
\(\El\,\iota\) to discharge \pp's arithmetic rules. The \emph{only} outright
axiom is \(\trust : \Pi P,\ \Prf\,P\) (\autoref{sec:trust}); everything else
is a standard-library primitive or a lemma proved from it. This kernel is
the entire trusted base against which reconstructed proofs are checked.

\subsection{Rules as soundness-proved symbols}
We encode each \pp{} inference rule as a \lp{} symbol whose \emph{dependent
type expresses the rule's soundness}: its premises become the symbol's
argument types and its conclusion the return type, each wrapped in
\(\Prf\). The symbol's body is a proof term---written as a tactic
script---that derives the conclusion from the premises using only the
kernel. Because \lp{} type-checks the body, the rule is \emph{proved}
sound rather than assumed. This is the key difference from encodings that
postulate each rule as an axiom and verify only its well-typedness, as is
done when reconstructing SMT proofs through hand-written per-rule
lemmas~\cite{coltellacciReconstructionSMTProofs2024a,mohamed25leansmt}:
here the per-rule justification is itself a checked object.

\autoref{fig:and2} shows the rule \rn{AND2}. Its body reads directly: from
a proof \(h_1\) of \(\bigwedge ps \Rightarrow \lnot P\) and the full
conjunction \(hc : \bigwedge(ps \mathbin{::} P)\), the term
\(h_1\,(\texttt{⋀\_init}\,hc)\,(\texttt{⋀\_last}\,hc)\) derives a
contradiction, which witnesses \(\lnot\bigwedge(ps \mathbin{::} P)\). The
same \(\mathbin{::}\)-pattern handles every conjunction rule uniformly,
whatever the arity \(n\), because \lp{} infers \(ps\) and \(P\) from the
goal.

\subsection{The shape of a rule}
A \pp{} rule appears in a replay as a bracketed name, and its shape is
decoded from that name by \tool's rule database (the single source of
truth for rule metadata):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{base} --- e.g. \rn{AND2}: a plain \refine{} against the
    encoded symbol.
  \item \emph{\(n\)-ary} (suffix \texttt{\_N}) --- e.g. \rn{ALL7\_2},
    \rn{NRM1\_3}: the suffix is a binder count, so the rule is applied
    with \(N\) bound variables. Quantifier and normalisation rules range
    over tuples of variables (\autoref{sec:infra}).
  \item \emph{result-form} (suffix \texttt{\_1}, ``primed'') --- e.g.
    \rn{AND2\_1}: a variant living in \(\Res\), the type of the
    equality-/rewrite-\emph{chain} \pp{} threads through a proof before a
    branching quantifier. Rather than a \(\Prf\)-proof, such a rule
    produces a \(\Res\,\varphi\): a term carrying the running goal's
    proof together with a proof that it equals \(\varphi\)
    (\(\mathsf{mk\_res}\), \(\mathsf{concat}\), \(\restm\)).
\end{itemize}
The two introduction rules that branch, \rn{ALL7} and \rn{XST8}, are
two-child: a \(\Res\)-typed chain establishing the body for an arbitrary
tuple witness, followed by a continuation under the bound variable. Their
types thread a per-tuple result \(\rho : \Pi v : \Tuple\,n,\ \Res(P\,v)\)
into the conclusion.

\subsection{Quantifiers and conjunction lists}\label{sec:infra}
Two pieces of infrastructure support the rules above. \emph{(i)~Conjunction
lists.} \pp's left-associative \(n\)-ary conjunction is represented as
\(\bigwedge : \mathbb{L}^{*}\,o \to \Prop\) over a snoc list of
propositions, with constructor \texttt{⋀\_intro} and projections
\texttt{⋀\_init}/\texttt{⋀\_last}; a unification rule lets a goal
\(\bigwedge(ps \mathbin{::} P)\) split structurally, so one rule matches all
arities. \emph{(ii)~Tuple quantifiers.} \pp's quantifiers range over lists
of variables, which we encode uniformly over \(\Tuple\,n\) (an \(n\)-tuple
of \(\El\,\iota\)) with projection \(\prj\) and tuple snoc. Four quantifier
formers appear: \(\allI\)/\(\xstI\) for the single-variable
\(\forall\)/\(\exists\) (over \(\Tuple\,1\)), and the \(n\)-ary
\(\Dia\)/\(\Hrt\)---\pp's \texttt{forall}/\texttt{forall2}, distinguished
by whether the quantified formula sits on the goal or the hypothesis side.
A single \(\Tuple\)-based rule then covers a whole family of arities, and
the emitter selects quantifier witnesses by tuple projection.

\subsection{Trust, audited}\label{sec:trust}
A few \pp{} steps are discharged by its internal solver rather than by
tracked inference: the replay records the conclusion but no checkable
justification. For these, the encoding passes the kernel's \(\trust\)
axiom \emph{as an argument} to an otherwise fully proved rule---never as a
whole-goal proof. Three categories occur:
(i)~\(\,\epsilon\,\)\,\textsc{bool} membership side-conditions (\pp{}
cannot reason about \(v : \textsc{bool}\) abstractly);
(ii)~the arithmetic-match conjuncts of the instantiation rule \rn{INS};
and (iii)~solver-confirmed numeric side-conditions of the arithmetic
rules (e.g. \rn{AR2}, \rn{AR13}). Each use is localized to one argument
and documented, and a pre-commit audit checks that no emitted proof
contains a bare whole-goal \(\refine\,\trust\). The trusted base is thus
exactly the \texttt{B.lp} kernel plus these enumerated, argument-level
uses.
